The History of Coffee

The beginning of coffee

While there are many legends about the origin of coffee, No one knows exactly how or when coffee was first discovered. One Ethiopian legend says that a goat herder named Kaldi first discovered the potential of these beloved beans. The story goes that that Kaldi discovered coffee after he noticed that after eating the berries from a certain tree, his goats became so energetic that they did not want to sleep at night. When he reported this to the monks they made a drink with the berries and found that it kept them alert through the long hours of evening prayer. The monks shared his discovery with the other monks at the monastery, and knowledge of the energizing berries began to spread. 1 As the world moved east, they brought coffee with them. The industrialization of coffee

European travelers brought back stories of an unusual dark black beverage. By the 17th century, coffee had made its way to Europe and was becoming popular across the country. Coffee quickly began to replace the common breakfast drink beverages of the time, beer and wine. Those who drank coffee in the morning instead of alcohol began the day alert and energized, and not surprisingly, the quality of their work was greatly improved. 12

Coffee came to Europe in the 17th century, it was brought there by European travelers. They brought back stories of an unusual dark black beverage. Coffee quickly began to replace the common breakfast drink beverages of the time, beer and wine. Those who drank coffee in the morning instead of alcohol began the day alert and energized. Coffee began to become very popular across Europe, by the mid 17th century there were over 300 coffee houses in London. 12

As Europe began to explore west, they brought coffee with them. In the late 1600s coffee was brought to New Amsterdam, later to called New York by the British. Although coffee houses began to appear in the ‘new world’ tea was still the most popular drink, which caused the Boston Tea Party. The Boston Tea Party and the taxes imposed by George III caused coffee to become the most popular drink in the “new world” by the late 17s and early 18th century. 12

This worldwide popularity there was a growing economic demand for more, and better coffee. The Dutch were the first to try and commercially produce coffee, first starting in India with no luck and then again in Indonesia with great success. Once they had successful production, they began to spread to other islands, Mexico and brazil. 12

Coffee In the World Today

Today coffee is still enjoyed everyday by billions of people. It is estimated that the average person spends between $1-$2k a year just on coffee.13 From my personal experience working at a coffee shop some people spend upwards of $15 a day, just on coffee. Coffee has such a prevalent part in our daily lives that some even be willing to consider it an addiction. Coffee is enjoyed in many different cultures and communities around the world.
