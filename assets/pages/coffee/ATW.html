Mexico


Quick Facts

  • Smaller coffee farms are more common than large plantations1
  • Still ranks one of the largest producers of coffee in the world1
  • Most farms are in the southern states1
  • Mexico produces an excellent bean for dark roasts1
  • Coffee rust problems significantly affect coffee production2
  • Coffee was cultivated in the 18th century by spain2

In Mexico coffee is an essential part of the culture. sobremesa is a time after a meal where people usually socialize and relax with coffee. Traditionally the coffee is served with tequila and other liqueur for the adults and spiced coffee like a cortados for the younger generation.1 Coffee taste differs by region, in the north they drink it with lots of sugar, the American way. In the southern states they don’t add as much sugar but add more spices compared to the north. Coffee was first introduced to the region in the 1700s as Mexico was colonized by Spain.2 Mexico produces very fruity flavored coffee but its also Chocolatey with a medium body and crisp acidity. The taste of the coffee depends heavily on the elevation and the climate

Puerto Rico


Quick Facts

  • Coffee was brought to Puerto Rico from Martinique in 1736 1
  • Produces beans noted for their balanced body and acidity, as well as a fruity aroma. 1
  • Coffee was introduced during Spanish colonial rule 3
  • Coffee is considered one of the basic needs in the culture 3
  • Some farmers focus on high-quality, specialty coffee 3

Coffee was first introduced to the region under Spanish colonial rule. When it was introduced it wasn’t considered a very important crop. In the 19th century population and hurricanes had a negative impact on coffee production. By 1950 coffee production couldn’t keep up with the demand on the island let alone exports. Today Puerto Rican coffee farmers focus more on high quality small batch specialty coffee as opposed to mass production for exports.3 This gives Puerto Rican coffee a very unique place in the world of coffee. Puerto Rican coffee traditionally has a balanced body and acidity, with a fruity aroma. This makes Puerto Rican coffee a rare sight to see, especially because they only produce about 17% of the coffee produced worldwide.4

Coffee was first introduced to the region in the 1800s to alleviate the decline in indigo and cochineal exports. By the late 20th century coffee accounted for 90% of the regions exports. Now Guatemala ranks 2nd in the world in terms of coffee production.6 Locals in Guatemala like to enjoy coffee from their own region as posed to importing it from other countries. Artisanal coffee has skyrocketed in the past decade with new coffee shops popping up all the time, all with the same focus, highlighting the great coffee Guatemala has to offer. 5Guatemalan coffee is traditionally more acidic than other regions, this id due to the volcanic soil and the elevation. This makes Guatemalan coffee perfect for espresso and dark roasts, as opposed to lighter roasts

Costa Rica


Quick Facts

  • Only produce Arabic coffee beans1
  • medium body and sharp acidity often described as the perfect balance 1
  • grown on mostly small farms1
  • Costa Rica has a reputation for fine coffee1

Coffee was first introduced to Costa Rica in the late 1700s when plants were gifted to anyone willing to grow them. The country has very mineral-rich volcanic soils and cool mountain climates these are the ideal conditions for growing coffee. Once Costa Rica had gained independence from Spain coffee exports helped fun lots of infrastructure in the country. But coffee also played a huge role in the culture as well everyone drank coffee from the farmer to the politician. The country only produces Arabica beans which are generally smoother and more balanced.7

Colombia


Quick Facts

  • Regarded as some of the highest quality in the world1
  • fahfhretraditionally grows arabica beans1
  • One of the largest coffee producers in the world1
  • Coffee was first introduced in the 1700s8
  • Produces a very diverse coffee flavor palette8

Beginning in the late 1700s Colombia quickly became a major exporter of coffee on the global scale. In 1977 the National Federation of Coffee Growers of Colombia was created to protect the interests of coffee growers, implementing a logistics system that enabled rural, small-estate farmers to export their coffee more efficiently. Since Colombia’s climate and elevation varies a lot the flavor of the coffee also varies with it. Lower and higher altitude coffee has a full body and deeper floral notes. While middle elevation tends to be well-rounded with nutty and chocolate flavors, a gentle sweetness, and a mellow acidity. Locals enjoy coffee much more than the rest of the world, they enjoy it at night not emphasizing the caffeine. This means coffee shops are open later and some aren’t even open in the mornings. 8

Some legends data coffee back to around 850 AD where a goat herder discovered the berries after his goats at them and became energized. Ethiopian coffee has a very diverse taste because of how many different plant varieties there are. Brazil


Quick Facts

  • Worlds largest coffee produce10
  • Commercially cultivated in the early 1800s10
  • Farmers have freedom to find their own buyers10
  • In the 1910s Brazil produced 80% of the worlds coffee10
  • Coffee has mild earthy notes as well as spicy, nutty, sweet with a low acidity10

Coffee was brought to the region in the early 1700s and early coffee production was mostly consumed locally. Around the mid 1800s a disease devastated the coffee industries of Asia, Brazil and South America the chance it needed to really thrive as coffee exporters. By the 1910s Brazil was producing 80% of the world coffee. In the 90s coffee was deregulated by the government allowing farmers to find their own buyers and sell how they wanted. This opened the gates for Brazil to become the work leader in coffee innovation. Today Brazil doesn’t produce nearly as large of a share of the worlds coffee, sitting at about 30% but they are still the largest producer in the world. Not only is Brazil the number one producer but they are also the number one consumer of coffee in the world, passing the United states in 2010. 10

indonesia


Quick Facts

  • Coffee was brought by Dutch traders in the late 1600s 11
  • One of the first countries to start growing coffee 11
  • 4th biggest producer in the world 11
  • 90% of coffee grown is by family farms 11
  • Coffee tends to have a bold taste with low acidity with earth, spice, and forestry notes 11

Coffee was first brought to the country by Dutch traders in the late 1600s. They smuggled the seeds from Yemen and brought them to Indonesia. Indonesia saw coffee plantations spread quickly and by the early 1700s commercial production was underway. Indonesia quickly because Europe’s preferred source for coffee beans. In the mid-1800s a disease knows as Coffee rust affected the crops destroying plantations on the region. To save the industry the Dutch planted different disease-resistant coffee species, robusta. Unfortunately, the farms were plagued by poverty, starvation, and inhumane working conditions. By 1942 the Dutch were forced from Indonesia and that significantly improved working conditions. When the Dutch left the government gave the plantations to the laborers giving them an opportunity to grow the industry. 11
